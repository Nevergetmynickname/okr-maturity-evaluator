# OKR Maturity Rubric

## Dimension 1: Strategic Alignment

Score 1:
The OKR is disconnected from higher-level strategy.

Score 2:
The OKR has a general relationship to strategy but the connection is implicit.

Score 3:
The OKR supports a clear business priority, but the relationship across levels is not fully explicit.

Score 4:
The OKR clearly connects strategy, department goals, and team execution.

Score 5:
The OKR creates strong vertical and horizontal alignment and helps teams make trade-off decisions.

## Dimension 2: Objective Quality

Score 1:
The Objective is a task, project name, or slogan.

Score 2:
The Objective describes a broad intention but lacks focus or outcome orientation.

Score 3:
The Objective is meaningful but may still mix activity and outcome.

Score 4:
The Objective is clear, focused, directional, and outcome-oriented.

Score 5:
The Objective is strategically sharp and inspires focused execution.

## Dimension 3: KR Measurability

Score 1:
KRs are mostly tasks or descriptions.

Score 2:
Some KRs are measurable, but they lack baseline, target, or business meaning.

Score 3:
Most KRs are measurable, but some are output or milestone based.

Score 4:
KRs are measurable, outcome-oriented, and supported by clear target values.

Score 5:
KRs combine outcome, quality, efficiency, and adoption indicators with clear thresholds.

## Dimension 4: Execution Grip

Score 1:
There is no clear mechanism to manage delivery.

Score 2:
Tasks exist, but they are not connected to leading indicators or risks.

Score 3:
Execution can be tracked, but mainly through milestone completion.

Score 4:
The OKR has leading indicators, checkpoints, owners, and process measures.

Score 5:
Execution grip supports early warning, trade-off decisions, and cross-functional coordination.

## Dimension 5: Ownership and Accountability

Score 1:
No clear owner or contributor.

Score 2:
Owner exists but accountability boundaries are unclear.

Score 3:
Owner and contributors are mostly clear.

Score 4:
Ownership, contribution, and dependencies are clear.

Score 5:
Shared accountability is designed for cross-functional outcomes.

## Dimension 6: Review and Governance

Score 1:
No review mechanism.

Score 2:
Review exists but is informal or activity-based.

Score 3:
Regular review exists but focuses mainly on progress reporting.

Score 4:
Review cadence, evidence, and escalation are clear.

Score 5:
Governance supports learning, prioritization, and management decisions.

## Common OKR Gaps

### Gap: KR is actually a task

Example:
"Complete supplier database cleanup."

Why it matters:
Completion does not prove business impact.

Better:
"Increase supplier master data accuracy from 82% to 95% by Q3."

### Gap: No baseline and target

Example:
"Improve delivery efficiency."

Why it matters:
Cannot evaluate challenge level or success.

Better:
"Reduce average delivery cycle time from 12 days to 8 days."

### Gap: Objective is too large

Example:
"Become industry-leading in operational excellence."

Why it matters:
Too broad to guide quarterly execution.

Better:
"Improve procurement execution reliability for high-priority delivery projects."

### Gap: Missing process grip

Example:
"Achieve 95% on-time delivery."

Why it matters:
This is a lagging result. The team needs leading indicators.

Possible process grips:
- Weekly blocked-order review
- Supplier risk heatmap
- Exception aging dashboard
- Escalation for orders delayed more than 5 working days

### Gap: Measurement coordinate shift

Example:
Department KR:
"Improve customer onboarding cycle time."

Individual KR:
"Complete 10 onboarding documents."

Why it matters:
The individual KR measures document output, not cycle-time improvement.

## Dimension 7: Balance Between Goal and Growth

Score 1:
The OKR is highly one-sided. It pushes one result aggressively but ignores quality, risk, capability, customer experience, or sustainability.

Score 2:
The OKR shows some awareness of balance, but trade-offs are not explicitly managed.

Score 3:
The OKR includes some balancing considerations, but they are incomplete or not measurable.

Score 4:
The OKR clearly balances short-term results with quality, capability, risk, and sustainable execution.

Score 5:
The OKR functions as a balanced management system. It helps leadership achieve ambitious goals while protecting long-term growth, organizational health, customer trust, and operational resilience.

### Gap: OKR pushes speed but ignores quality

Example:
"Reduce onboarding cycle time from 10 days to 3 days."

Potential hidden loss:
The team may speed up the process by skipping risk checks, reducing communication quality, or increasing downstream rework.

Better:
"Reduce onboarding cycle time from 10 days to 6 days while maintaining customer satisfaction above 90% and reducing rework rate below 5%."

### Gap: OKR pushes cost reduction but ignores resilience

Example:
"Reduce procurement cost by 15%."

Potential hidden loss:
The team may select cheaper suppliers, but supplier quality, delivery reliability, or long-term partnership strength may suffer.

Better:
"Reduce procurement cost by 10% while maintaining supplier delivery reliability above 95% and quality issue rate below 3%."

### Gap: OKR pushes output but ignores team capability

Example:
"Launch 10 new AI use cases this quarter."

Potential hidden loss:
The team may produce many pilots, but without adoption, governance, reusable assets, or operating capability.

Better:
"Launch 5 validated AI use cases with adoption above 60%, documented governance review, and reusable implementation playbook."
